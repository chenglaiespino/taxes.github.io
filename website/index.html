Evolution of Taxation: Insights from Philippine History

Taxation in the Philippines has undergone significant changes throughout history, mirroring the country's economic, social, and political shifts. From the pre-colonial era, where tributes and trade taxes existed, to the Spanish colonization introducing tribute taxes, land taxes, and forced labor. Over time, tax policies have evolved, aiming for fairer distribution, increased compliance, and sustainability in funding vital public services and infrastructure development

Precolonial Era

Precolonial Era

There was a system of taxation called buwis or handug, which was a form of payment in goods or crops, that the people living under the datu had to give in exchange for protection and security. This system was based on the barter economy that was prevalent at the time. The timawa (freemen) were the ones who paid taxes, as they were the ones who benefited from the protection of the datu. The maharlikas (nobles) and the datu themselves were not required to pay taxes, as they were the ones who provided protection. The oripun (slaves) were also exempt from taxation, as they were not considered to be part of the social hierarchy. This system was based on the belief that those who benefited from the protection of the datu should contribute to the upkeep of the barangay. It was a form of mutual obligation between the ruler and the ruled. In addition to the buwis or handug, there were also other forms of taxation in pre-colonial Philippines. For example, there was the upahan, which was a form of labor service that was required of all men. The datu could also impose fines for violations of the laws of the barangay. These fines were often paid in goods or animals. The system of taxation in pre-colonial Philippines was a complex one that reflected the social and political structure of the time. It was a system that was based on the barter economy and the belief that those who benefited from the protection of the datu should contribute to its upkeep. This system continued to exist for centuries, even after the arrival of the Spaniards in 1521.

Spanish Era

Spanish Era

The encomienda system was a central feature of Spanish colonial rule in the Philippines. It granted Spanish conquistadors, officials, or favored individuals the right to extract tribute and labor from indigenous communities. While initially intended to support soldiers and officials, the system quickly transformed into a form of exploitation, with encomenderos abusing their power and imposing excessive burdens on the indigenous population. Tribute, a form of tax imposed on indigenous communities, was initially set at 8 to 10 reales but was often collected in goods or forced labor. This practice, known as "polo y servicio," further exacerbated the hardships faced by the indigenous people. Over time, the tribute system was replaced with the cedula, a certificate or identification issued to individuals who could pay a fixed amount of tax. This change aimed to improve tax collection efficiency and maintain better records of taxpayers. The encomienda system, with its intertwined taxation, labor obligations, and tribute payments, left a lasting legacy of exploitation and hardship for indigenous communities. It played a significant role in shaping the economic and social landscape of the Philippines during the Spanish colonial era.

American Era

American Era (1989 - 1901)

During the early American regime in the Philippines (1898-1901), the country was initially governed by American military leaders. It wasn't until 1902 that the first civil government was established under William H. Taft. However, the creation of the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) occurred during the term of the second civil governor, Luke E. Wright, through the passage of Reorganization Act No. 1189 on July 2, 1904. The formal establishment of the BIR took place on August 1, 1904, operating under the Secretary of Finance, Henry Ide, who authored the Internal Revenue Law of 1904. John S. Hord served as the first Collector (later known as Commissioner) of the BIR, leading an initial staff of 69 employees, including a Collector, Vice-Collector, Chief Clerk, Law Clerk, Records Clerk, and three Division Chiefs.

Subsequently, after John S. Hord's tenure, the BIR was led by three more American collectors: Ellis Cromwell (1909-1912), William T. Holting (1912-1914), and James J. Rafferty (1914-1918). These appointments were made by the Governor-General with the approval of the Philippine Commission and the U.S. President. Regarding taxation, the establishment of the Bureau of Internal Revenue marked a crucial development in the Philippines' fiscal system under American rule. The creation of the BIR aimed to streamline and regulate taxation, implementing the Internal Revenue Law of 1904 authored by Henry Ide. This law laid the foundation for a formal system of taxation in the Philippines, introducing various tax structures and guidelines for revenue collection, marking a shift toward more organized fiscal governance.

Some of the key provisions of the Internal Revenue Law of 1904 included:

  • A graduated income tax, with rates ranging from 2% to 4%
  • A tax on real estate
  • A tax on tobacco products
  • A tax on spirits and fermented liquors

Japanese Era

Japanese Era (1942 - 1945)

The Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) was combined with the Customs Office and was headed by a Director of Customs and Internal Revenue. This change was made to consolidate the taxation and customs functions of the government under one agency. The Japanese occupation of the Philippines brought about significant changes to the country's tax system. The Japanese government imposed a number of new taxes, including a head tax, a land tax, and a business tax. These taxes were used to finance the war effort and to support the Japanese administration in the Philippines. The Japanese taxation system was more centralized and controlled than the American system that had preceded it. The Japanese government also used a variety of coercive measures to collect taxes, including forced labor and the threat of violence. The Japanese occupation had a devastating impact on the Philippines' economy and on the lives of its people. The war caused widespread destruction and disruption, and the heavy taxation imposed by the Japanese government further exacerbated the suffering of the Filipino people. After the war, the Philippine government embarked on a process of rebuilding the country's economy and restoring peace and order. The taxation system was one of the areas that required significant reform. The BIR was separated from the Customs Office, and a new system of taxation was introduced.

Post-War Era to Present

Post-War Era to Present

Apologies for the length. Here's a more concise summary in 10 sentences: Following independence in 1946, the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) underwent significant reorganizations for a more efficient tax system. Executive Order No. 94 in 1947 merged divisions and centralized legal functions. Revenue Regulations No. V-2 established 31 inspection units overseen by Revenue Agents. In 1951, Executive Order No. 392 created Legal, Assessment, and Collection departments. Memorandum Order No. V-188 introduced the withholding tax system under Republic Act 690, collecting 25% of income tax upon receipt, altering tax collection methods. In 1954 (RMO No. 41), new offices like the Specific Tax Division were formed. A Training Unit was established in 1954 for skill development. Decentralization efforts in 1955 led to Regional Offices in Cebu and Davao, managed by Regional Directors overseeing five Branches each. Overall, these reorganizations aimed to enhance the BIR's structure, introduce specialized divisions, and improve tax collection nationwide.

Ferdinand Marcos Administration (1965-1986)

During Misael Vera's tenure as Commissioner in 1965, the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) embraced innovative programs like the "Blue Master Program" and the "Voluntary Tax Compliance Program," aiming to curb abuses and encourage accurate reporting of income. Vera's administration subdivided the country into 20 Regional Offices and 90 Revenue District Offices while establishing offices such as the Internal Audit Department, Administrative Service Department, International Tax Affairs Staff, and Specific Tax Department. Initiatives like assigning taxpayers permanent Tax Account Numbers (TAN) and enabling tax payments through banks, along with package audit investigations, expedited tax verification and contributed to enhanced collection performance. The proclamation of Martial Law in 1972 introduced new government approaches, including tax amnesty decrees for erring taxpayers. Notably, under Commissioner Efren Plana in 1976, the BIR's National Office relocated to its dedicated building in Quezon City, coinciding with President Marcos's promulgation of the National Internal Revenue Code of 1977. In 1980, Commissioner Ruben Ancheta oversaw further reorganization to enhance the BIR's responsiveness to the public's tax needs.

Corazon Aquino Administration (1986-1992)

During the Cory Aquino administration from post-1986 to Commissioner Jose Ong's term in 1989, the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) initiated crucial reforms. "Operation: Walang Lagay" ensured transparent tax collection. Executive Order No. 127 in January 1987 restructured the BIR into Assessment and Collection and Legal and Internal Administration divisions. The introduction of the value-added tax (VAT) in 1988 simplified tax systems, backed by a compliance campaign. The 1986 Tax Reform Program and absorbing RISSI back into the BIR in 1988 further impacted tax assessment computerization. Commissioner Jose Ong's tenure in 1989 ushered in the "Tax Administration Program," implementing the Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN) and the New Payment Control System, modernizing tax administration to streamline processes and encourage compliance.

Fidel V. Ramos Administration (1992-1998)

During the Ramos administration, Commissioner Liwayway Vinzons-Chato led the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) and introduced the Action-Centered Transformation Program (ACTS) in 1993 to align the organization with its vision. Under her leadership, the five-year Tax Computerization Project (TCP) began in 1994, aiming to modernize the tax system with an Integrated Tax System and Internal Administration System. In July 1997, Executive Order No. 430 was enacted to streamline the BIR in support of the computerized Integrated Tax System. This EO established the Legal and Enforcement Group as the fourth Revenue Group, led by a Deputy Commissioner. Additionally, it created various service units like the Internal Affairs Service, Taxpayers Assistance Service, Information Planning and Quality Service, and Revenue Data Centers. These initiatives began with Commissioner Chato's term in 1993 and continued until the passage of Executive Order No. 430 in July 1997. They focused on modernizing tax systems, enhancing service delivery, and restructuring the BIR for more effective tax administration.

Joseph Estrada Administration (1998-2001)

During President Estrada's administration, Commissioners Rualo and later Fonacier initiated vital reforms at the BIR. Rualo introduced the ERAP Program, granting audit immunity to taxpayers paying 20% more tax than 1997. He promoted receipt collection through the "Humingi ng Resibo, Manalo ng Libo-Libo" raffle and established the Large Taxpayers Monitoring System. Fonacier prioritized compliance by enhancing tax computerization, expanding electronic stamp tax metering, and implementing a Compromise Settlement Program for overdue accounts. Collaborations with local government leagues and private organizations supported tax campaigns. Executive Orders 175 and 306 established and later integrated the Large Taxpayers Service (LTS) and Excise Taxpayers Service (ETS). The Electronic Commerce Act in 2000 prompted the Full Integrated Tax System (ITS) Rollout Acceleration Program, advancing tax computerization with seven ITS back-end systems. These reforms spanned Rualo's initiatives to Fonacier's term, focusing on compliance, technological upgrades, and strengthening tax administration in the Philippines during President Estrada's tenure.

Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo Administration (2001-2010)

During Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo's presidency from 2001 to 2009, the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) underwent substantial reforms aimed at bolstering tax collection and streamlining administration. These reforms included pivotal programs such as the "Taxpayer-Focused BIR," which simplified the tax code and reduced tax rates, alongside initiatives like the "Voluntary Assessment Program (VAP)" and "Compromise Settlement Program (CSP)," encouraging voluntary disclosure of under-declared income. The introduction of electronic services such as the Electronic Filing and Payment System (eFPS) modernized tax procedures. Moreover, programs like the "Run After Tax Evaders (RATE)" and "Oplan Kandado" focused on identifying tax evaders, closing non-compliant businesses, and intensifying collections. The BIR also implemented measures for enhanced taxpayer service, such as BIR Contact Centers, eLounges, and a "Taxpayer Feedback Mechanism." These comprehensive initiatives significantly improved tax collection, simplified the tax system, and aimed for greater fairness, transparency, and efficiency.

Benigno Aquino III Administration (2010-2016)

After the inauguration of President Benigno C. Aquino III on June 30, 2010, Atty. Kim S. Jacinto-Henares, a former BIR Deputy Commissioner, assumed the position of the new Commissioner of Internal Revenue. Commissioner Henares dedicated her initial months in the BIR to prioritize the filing of tax evasion cases as part of the RATE Program, aligning her efforts with President Aquino's State of the Nation Address (SONA) directives. Her focus on pursuing tax evasion cases aimed to uphold accountability and compliance within the Philippine tax system, echoing the administration's commitment to fostering fairness and transparency. Henares' proactive stance in targeting tax evasion underscored the Aquino administration's resolve to strengthen tax enforcement mechanisms and ensure adherence to tax regulations for the country's fiscal integrity. Through strategic initiatives and enforcement measures, Henares aimed to uphold the principles of integrity and accountability in tax collection during her tenure as the BIR Commissioner.

Rodrigo Duterte Administration (2016-2022)

On December 19, 2017, President Rodrigo Duterte signed into law the Tax Reform for Acceleration and Inclusion (TRAIN) Law. This landmark legislation introduced significant changes to the Philippine tax system, including providing tax exemptions for low-income earners and increasing excise taxes on certain goods. The TRAIN Law aimed to promote inclusivity and equity in the tax system, while also generating additional revenue for the government to fund various social programs and infrastructure projects. The law's implementation led to an increase in prices for petroleum products and some consumer goods, but it also provided relief to low-income households by exempting them from paying income taxes. Overall, the TRAIN Law represented a major step towards a more progressive and fair tax system in the Philippines. The TRAIN Law, a pivotal policy under President Duterte's administration, sought to balance tax relief for low-income individuals with increased taxes on specific commodities to bolster government revenue. While it faced initial challenges such as price hikes, particularly in fuel and select goods, the law's core goal of enhancing social welfare and building a fairer tax structure remained integral to its implementation strategy.
